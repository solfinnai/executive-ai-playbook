Chapter 1

THE SUBTRACTION AUDIT

Before you add AI to anything, you need to understand what you're actually doing.

Most executives can't tell me how they spend their time. They know they're "busy," but they can't break down the 40-60 hours per week into specific, automatable tasks.

This is a problem. You can't optimize what you can't measure. You can't automate what you can't define.

The Subtraction Audit fixes this. It's a systematic inventory of your operation that identifies exactly which tasks can be automated, delegated, or eliminated. By the end of this chapter, you'll know:

  • Which 20% of your tasks create 80% of your value
  • Which tasks are prime candidates for AI automation
  • How much time and money you could save by subtracting instead of adding
  • What your organization would look like if you removed all automatable work

Why Most People Get This Wrong

The typical approach to AI implementation is additive: "Let's get ChatGPT for the marketing team." "Let's try an AI scheduling assistant." "Let's pilot an AI customer service bot."

This creates AI sprawl: a dozen tools that marginally improve existing processes without fundamentally changing how you operate. You end up with higher tool costs, more complexity, and minimal transformation.

AI-native companies take the opposite approach. They subtract before they add. They identify what doesn't need to happen at all, then use AI to handle what remains.

The goal isn't to do what you're doing now, but faster. The goal is to stop doing most of what you're doing now, and use AI to handle what actually matters.

The Framework: RAISE Analysis

Every task in your business falls into one of five categories:

R - Remove: Tasks that add no value and can be eliminated entirely
A - Automate: Repeatable tasks that AI can handle better/faster
I - Improve: Tasks that humans should keep doing, but AI can enhance
S - Simplify: Complex tasks that need to be broken down before automation
E - Elevate: High-value tasks where humans add unique value

Remove (10-20% of tasks)

These are tasks that exist because "we've always done them," not because they create value.

Common examples:

  • Status meetings where no decisions are made
  • Reports that nobody reads or acts on
  • Approval processes for routine decisions
  • Redundant quality checks
  • Manual data entry that duplicates existing systems

The best AI implementation starts with elimination. Don't automate what shouldn't happen at all.

Automate (30-50% of tasks)

These are the sweet spot for AI: repetitive, rule-based tasks that consume time without requiring judgment.

Prime candidates:

  • Email triage and basic responses
  • Calendar scheduling and coordination
  • Data analysis and reporting
  • Content creation from templates
  • Customer support for common questions
  • Invoice processing and basic accounting
  • Lead qualification and routing

For each automatable task, estimate:

  • Hours per week currently spent
  • Hourly cost (salary + overhead)
  • Annual cost to keep doing manually
  • Estimated automation cost
  • Annual savings from automation

Improve (20-30% of tasks)

These are tasks that require human oversight but can be dramatically enhanced by AI assistance.

Examples:

  • Strategic analysis (AI gathers data, human interprets)
  • Creative writing (AI drafts, human edits)
  • Sales calls (AI researches prospects, human builds relationships)
  • Hiring decisions (AI screens resumes, human interviews)
  • Product development (AI generates options, human chooses direction)

The key is partnership: AI handles preparation and routine tasks, humans handle judgment and relationships.

Simplify (5-15% of tasks)

These are complex, multi-step processes that need to be broken down before automation becomes possible.

Examples:

  • Project management workflows
  • Custom client deliverables
  • Strategic planning processes
  • Vendor evaluation and selection

For these tasks, map the process step-by-step, then apply RAISE analysis to each step individually.

Elevate (10-20% of tasks)

These are high-leverage activities where human insight, creativity, and judgment create disproportionate value.

Examples:

  • Strategic decision-making
  • Key relationship building
  • Creative vision and direction
  • Crisis management
  • Cultural leadership
  • Innovation and R&D direction

AI might provide support (research, analysis, options), but humans drive these activities. Protect time for elevation work by automating everything else.

Running Your Subtraction Audit

Here's the practical process:

Step 1: Time Tracking (1 week)

Track every task you do for one full week. Use whatever method works (calendar blocking, time-tracking app, or simple notepad), but be specific:

  • "Email" → "Read and respond to vendor proposals"
  • "Meeting" → "Weekly team status meeting"
  • "Planning" → "Draft Q3 budget projections"

For each task, note:

  • Time spent
  • Level of mental effort required (1-10)
  • Whether it's repeatable/consistent
  • Value created (revenue, decisions, relationships)

Don't change your behavior during tracking week. Track what you actually do, not what you think you should do. The goal is an honest baseline.

Step 2: Categorization (2 hours)

Group similar tasks and apply RAISE analysis:

Task Hours/Week RAISE Category Action Time Savings Potential
Email responses 8 Automate AI drafts, human reviews 6 hours
Weekly status meetings 3 Remove Replace with async updates 3 hours
Financial analysis 4 Improve AI data pull, human insights 2 hours

Step 3: ROI Calculation (1 hour)

For tasks in the "Automate" and "Improve" categories, calculate the economics:

Current Annual Cost:
(Hours per week × Hourly cost × 52 weeks)

Automation Annual Cost:
(Tool costs + Setup time + Monitoring time)

Net Annual Savings:
Current cost - Automation cost

Payback Period:
Setup costs ÷ Monthly savings

Step 4: Prioritization

Rank automation opportunities by:

  1. Quick wins (high savings, low setup cost)
  2. Volume plays (medium savings, but high frequency)
  3. Strategic bets (transformational impact, higher complexity)

Start with quick wins to build momentum and credibility.

Common Audit Findings

After conducting dozens of these audits, patterns emerge:

Email consumes 20-35% of executive time
Most of it is automatable. AI can draft responses, prioritize messages, and extract action items. Executives should spend 5-10% of their time on email, not 25%.

Meetings are the biggest time sink
50-60% of meetings have no clear outcome. 30% can be replaced with async communication. The remainder can be shortened with AI-generated agendas and summaries.

Reporting and analysis is largely automatable
Most executives spend 10-15% of their time creating or reviewing reports. AI can generate 90% of these reports automatically. Humans should focus on interpreting data, not compiling it.

Administrative tasks compound at scale
Scheduling, expense tracking, document management, and other admin tasks grow linearly with team size. AI can handle most of these with minimal oversight.

ROI REALITY CHECK

Most executives approach AI ROI backwards. They focus on technology costs instead of opportunity costs. They calculate savings instead of value creation. They think about AI as an expense instead of infrastructure.

The Traditional ROI Trap

Here's how most executives calculate AI ROI:

Traditional Calculation:
Labor cost saved - Technology cost = ROI

Example: "If we automate email management, we'll save 10 hours/week. At $50/hour, that's $26,000/year in savings. The AI tool costs $5,000/year. ROI = $21,000."

This calculation isn't wrong, but it's incomplete. It misses the three sources of AI's real value:

  1. Velocity gains: Decisions made in minutes instead of hours
  2. Scale effects: Handling 10x volume without 10x costs
  3. Capability expansion: Doing things that weren't possible before

The AI Value Framework

AI creates value in four ways:

1. Cost Reduction (Traditional ROI)

This is what most people focus on: doing the same work with fewer people or less time.

Examples:

  • Automating data entry
  • AI customer service for routine inquiries
  • Automated report generation

Typical ROI: 200-500% in year 1

2. Quality Improvement

AI often performs tasks more consistently and accurately than humans, especially for repetitive work.

Examples:

  • Fewer errors in data processing
  • More consistent customer service responses
  • Better compliance with processes and regulations

Value: Hard to quantify, but reduces risk and customer complaints

3. Velocity Acceleration

AI can complete certain tasks in seconds that take humans hours or days.

Examples:

  • Market research that takes 2 minutes instead of 2 days
  • Contract analysis in minutes instead of hours
  • Customer inquiry response in seconds instead of hours

Value: Faster decisions, better customer experience, competitive advantage

4. Scale Enablement

AI allows you to handle much larger volumes without proportional cost increases.

Examples:

  • Supporting 10x more customers with the same team size
  • Analyzing 100x more data points
  • Creating 50x more content variations

Value: Enables growth that wouldn't otherwise be possible

The Hidden Costs of AI Implementation

Most ROI calculations underestimate implementation costs. Here's what actually gets expensive:

1. Integration Complexity

  • API integration: $5,000-25,000 depending on systems
  • Data formatting: $3,000-15,000 to clean and structure data
  • Workflow redesign: $10,000-50,000 in consultant/internal time

2. Change Management

  • Training: $2,000-5,000 per team member
  • Process documentation: $5,000-20,000
  • Productivity loss during transition: 20-30% for 2-4 weeks

3. Ongoing Maintenance

  • AI monitoring and optimization: 5-10 hours/month
  • Tool upgrades and migrations: $2,000-10,000/year
  • Security and compliance: $5,000-25,000/year

Budget 2-3x your initial estimate for AI implementation. Most projects cost more than expected, but also deliver more value than expected.

When NOT to Implement AI

AI isn't always the answer. Avoid AI implementation when:

1. The Process Itself Is Broken

Don't automate bad processes. If your current workflow is inefficient, fix the workflow first, then consider automation.

Example: A company wanted to automate their vendor approval process. The process took 6 weeks and involved 12 people. The problem wasn't automation—it was that they needed 12 approvals for a $500 purchase.

2. High-Stakes, Low-Volume Tasks

If a task happens rarely but mistakes are expensive, keep it human-driven.

Example: Executive hiring decisions. You might use AI to screen resumes, but humans should make the final calls.

3. Rapidly Changing Requirements

If the task requirements change frequently, automation creates more work than it saves.

Example: Early-stage startup marketing. The messaging, channels, and audience change monthly. Build sustainable processes before automating them.

4. High-Touch Relationship Work

Tasks that depend on personal relationships and context shouldn't be automated.

Example: Key client relationship management, crisis communication, sensitive negotiations.

ROI Measurement Framework

Here's how to measure AI ROI correctly:

Before Implementation

Establish baselines for:

  • Time metrics: How long does X currently take?
  • Cost metrics: What does X currently cost?
  • Quality metrics: How often do errors occur?
  • Capacity metrics: What's the maximum volume you can handle?

During Implementation (First 90 Days)

Track:

  • Setup costs: All implementation expenses
  • Time to value: When did you start seeing benefits?
  • Adoption rate: What percentage of the process is automated?
  • Error rate: How often does the AI need human correction?

After Implementation (Monthly)

Measure:

  • Direct savings: Time/cost reduction from automation
  • Quality improvement: Reduction in errors or customer complaints
  • Velocity gains: Faster completion times
  • Scale effects: Increased capacity without proportional cost increase

The Compound ROI Effect

The best AI implementations create compound returns:

Month 1-3: Direct cost savings (traditional ROI)
Month 4-6: Quality and velocity improvements
Month 7-12: Scale effects enable new business opportunities
Year 2+: Competitive advantage compounds

Companies that understand this continue investing in AI even after achieving their initial ROI targets. Companies that don't often stop optimizing once they hit their first savings goal.

ROI Optimization Strategies

1. Start with High-Frequency, Low-Complexity Tasks

Email processing, data entry, and scheduling automation deliver fast ROI because:

  • Setup is relatively simple
  • Volume is high
  • Success is easy to measure

2. Focus on Your Bottlenecks

Automate the tasks that limit your growth. If customer service response time is constraining sales, start there. If manual reporting is slowing decision-making, automate that.

3. Measure Everything

You can't optimize what you don't measure. Track time saved, quality improvements, and capacity increases. Most companies underestimate their AI ROI because they only track direct costs.

4. Reinvest the Savings

Don't just pocket the savings. Reinvest them in additional automation or higher-value activities. Compound the benefits.

THE STRATEGIC AI FRAMEWORK

Most companies approach AI tactically: "Let's try this tool." "Let's automate that process." "Let's see what happens."

The AI Advantage Stack

AI creates competitive advantage at four levels:

Level 1: Operational Efficiency

Using AI to do existing work faster and cheaper.

Examples: Automated customer service, expense processing, report generation

Competitive impact: Lower costs, faster execution

Defensibility: Low (competitors can copy quickly)

Level 2: Enhanced Decision Making

Using AI to make better decisions with better data.

Examples: Predictive analytics, market intelligence, risk assessment

Competitive impact: Better strategic choices, faster adaptation

Defensibility: Medium (requires data and expertise)

Level 3: Product/Service Innovation

Using AI to create new products or dramatically improve existing ones.

Examples: AI-powered features, personalization, new service offerings

Competitive impact: Revenue growth, market differentiation

Defensibility: High (first-mover advantage)

Level 4: Business Model Innovation

Using AI to create entirely new ways of creating and capturing value.

Examples: Platform businesses, data monetization, AI-as-a-service

Competitive impact: Market disruption, new industries

Defensibility: Very high (network effects, switching costs)

Most companies get stuck at Level 1. Winners climb to Level 3-4.

Building AI-Native Competitive Moats

Traditional competitive advantages (brand, distribution, scale) remain important, but AI creates new types of moats:

1. Data Network Effects

The more customers use your AI-powered product, the better it gets, which attracts more customers.

Example: Netflix's recommendation algorithm improves with every viewing decision, making the service more valuable to users and harder for competitors to replicate.

How to build: Create AI products where user activity generates training data that improves the product.

2. AI Talent Concentration

Companies that attract top AI talent can iterate faster and build more sophisticated systems.

How to build: Invest in AI education for existing team, create interesting AI projects that attract talent, partner with universities.

3. Proprietary Training Data

Companies with unique, high-quality training data can build AI systems competitors can't replicate.

Example: Medical AI companies with exclusive access to patient data from major health systems.

How to build: Identify your unique data assets, create exclusive data partnerships, invest in data quality and labeling.

4. AI-Native Process Design

Companies that redesign processes around AI capabilities rather than bolt AI onto existing processes.

How to build: Question every assumption about "how work gets done," design processes that maximize AI leverage.

5. Speed of AI Innovation

Companies that can rapidly experiment, learn, and scale AI applications.

How to build: Create AI experimentation infrastructure, establish rapid prototyping processes, build internal AI platforms.

  • Value multiplication: Each piece of work creates compound value

  • System learns from conversion patterns to optimize sequences

Component 4: Feedback Loops

This is what separates scale engines from automation. Feedback loops make systems smarter over time.

Types of Feedback Loops:

  • Performance Feedback: Track success metrics and optimize accordingly
  • User Feedback: Learn from human inputs and corrections
  • Market Feedback: Adapt to changing customer preferences
  • System Feedback: AI systems improve other AI systems

Implementation:

  • Every AI output includes quality scoring
  • Human corrections are fed back to improve future outputs
  • Success metrics automatically adjust system parameters
  • Regular analysis identifies patterns for optimization

Common 30-Day Challenges and Solutions

Challenge 1: Team Resistance

Symptom: Team members avoid using AI tools or revert to old methods

Solution: Make AI the easiest way to complete tasks, not an additional step

  • Integrate AI into existing workflows rather than creating new ones
  • Show immediate personal benefits for each team member
  • Start with AI that makes work easier, not that replaces people

Challenge 2: Poor AI Output Quality

Symptom: AI generates low-quality or irrelevant responses

Solution: Improve prompts and provide better context

  • Be specific about desired outputs and format
  • Provide examples of good outputs
  • Include relevant context and constraints
  • Iterate and refine prompts based on results

Challenge 3: Integration Difficulties

Symptom: AI tools don't connect properly with existing systems

Solution: Use API-first tools and build robust connections

  • Choose tools with strong API support
  • Use middleware platforms like Make or Zapier
  • Test integrations thoroughly before full deployment
  • Have technical support available for integration issues

Challenge 4: Scaling Too Fast

Symptom: Systems break under increased load or usage

Solution: Scale gradually and monitor performance

  • Start with small pilot groups
  • Monitor system performance and usage patterns
  • Scale in phases rather than all at once
  • Have rollback plans for each implementation

Best Practices for Prompt Optimization

Making Prompts More Effective

  • Be specific: Replace "analyze this" with "analyze market opportunities for SaaS companies in healthcare"
  • Provide context: Include relevant background information and constraints
  • Set expectations: Specify format, length, and level of detail needed
  • Include examples: Show what good output looks like when possible
  • Iterate: Refine prompts based on output quality

Common Prompt Mistakes

  • Too vague: "Help me with strategy" vs. "Analyze competitive positioning options"
  • No context: Not providing relevant background information
  • Wrong scope: Asking for too much or too little detail
  • Missing constraints: Not specifying limitations or requirements
  • Poor formatting: Unclear structure makes AI output harder to use

Remember: Great AI outputs start with great prompts. Invest time in crafting clear, specific, and well-structured prompts for the best results.

Appendix D

GLOSSARY

This glossary defines key terms used throughout the book. Understanding these concepts will help you communicate effectively with technical teams and AI vendors.

A

Agent — An AI system that can take actions on your behalf, not just answer questions. Unlike chatbots, agents can execute tasks, use tools, and work autonomously toward goals.

AI-Adopted vs. AI-Native — AI-adopted companies bolt AI tools onto existing processes. AI-native companies redesign processes around what AI does best. The difference is like comparing a electric car with a gas-engine interior to a car built from scratch for electric motors.

API (Application Programming Interface) — The plumbing that lets software talk to software. When you connect your email to Slack via Zapier, you're using APIs. Most modern AI tools have APIs so you can build custom integrations.

Automation — Getting AI or software to do tasks repeatedly without human input. Email filtering is automation. Invoice processing is automation. The best automation removes the task entirely, not just speeds it up.

C

Chatbot — An AI that responds to your text. ChatGPT is a chatbot. Most chatbots today are stateless—they don't remember previous conversations unless you explicitly give them context. This is why agents are better for complex tasks.

Claude — AI model made by Anthropic. Known for clear reasoning, long context windows, and honesty about limitations. Often preferred by executives for writing and strategic thinking.

Context Window — How much text an AI can "see" at once. Claude has 200K tokens (roughly 150K words). ChatGPT's free version sees only ~3K tokens. A larger context window means the AI can digest entire documents or long conversations without forgetting what came before.

D

Deep Learning — A branch of machine learning using neural networks with many layers. All modern AI (GPT, Claude, etc.) is deep learning. Executives don't need to know how it works—just that it's powerful and it requires lots of data and computing power.

E

Embedding — A mathematical representation of meaning. When an AI converts text into numbers that capture "what this text means," that's an embedding. Useful for semantic search ("find similar documents") and vector databases.

F

Few-Shot Learning — Showing an AI a few examples of what you want, and it learns from those examples. "Here are 3 examples of good meeting notes—now summarize this meeting." Better than zero-shot (no examples) but worse than fine-tuning.

Fine-Tuning — Training an AI model on your specific data so it learns your patterns. Expensive and usually unnecessary for executives. Most use-cases are better solved with prompt engineering or RAG.

Foundation Model — A large AI model trained on huge amounts of general data, then adapted for specific tasks. GPT-4, Claude, and Gemini are foundation models. The foundation is the expensive part; customization is cheap.

G

GPT (Generative Pre-trained Transformer) — OpenAI's family of AI models. GPT-4 is their best, GPT-3.5 powers ChatGPT's free version. "Generative" means it creates text; "pre-trained" means it learned from internet data; "Transformer" is the architecture (you don't need to know this part).

Guardrails — Rules you put on an AI to keep it safe and on-brand. "Never discuss pricing," "always mention our warranty," "refuse requests for illegal activities." Important for customer-facing AI.

H

Hallucination — When an AI confidently states something false. "What year did your company go public?" "2005" (but you never did). All current AI hallucinates. Best practice: use Claude for reasoning, Perplexity for fact-checking.

Human-in-the-Loop — A process where AI does most of the work, but a human reviews/approves before it matters. "AI drafts the contract, lawyer reviews it before sending." Smart for high-stakes decisions.

I

Inference — Running a trained AI model to generate new outputs. When you type into ChatGPT, that's inference. Different from training (which is expensive and slow) or fine-tuning (which is moderate cost).

K

Knowledge Graph — A structured way of storing facts and relationships. Instead of asking "who reports to whom?" (and the AI having to search through documents), a knowledge graph has "Alice → reports to → Bob" explicitly stored. Makes agents smarter and faster.

L

Large Language Model (LLM) — An AI trained on billions of words from the internet. GPT-4, Claude, Gemini, etc. are all LLMs. They're good at language tasks (writing, analysis, coding) and can do reasoning if prompted well.

LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation) — A clever way to customize an AI model for your specific task without retraining the whole thing. Cheaper than fine-tuning, not as good as full training. Niche use-case for most executives.

M

Machine Learning — AI that learns patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed. Instead of coding "if revenue > $1M, mark as high-value," you show examples and the ML model learns the pattern. Foundation for all modern AI.

Model Collapse — A phenomenon where AI trained on outputs from previous AI systems (rather than original human data) degrades over time. Real but over-hyped. Means: don't build AI on AI-generated data long-term.

Multimodal — An AI that can handle multiple types of input/output. GPT-4 with vision can read images and text. Claude can analyze PDFs, spreadsheets, and code. Multimodal is more powerful than text-only.

N

Natural Language Processing (NLP) — The field of AI focused on understanding and generating human language. When a tool extracts information from an email, that's NLP. When it generates a summary, that's NLP.

Neural Network — A mathematical structure inspired by how brains work, with interconnected "neurons." All modern AI uses neural networks. You don't need to understand the math; just know they're powerful and require lots of data to train.

O

Open Source AI — AI models you can download and run yourself (Llama, Mistral, etc.). Cheaper to run at scale than API-based models, but requires technical setup. Good for privacy-sensitive work or high-volume use.

OpenClaw — A framework for building AI agents that work with Claude. Open source, Claude-optimized, good for custom workflows. Lower-level than Zapier but more flexible.

Orchestration — Coordinating multiple AI calls to accomplish a complex task. "First, ask the AI to analyze this data. Then, feed that analysis into another AI call to draft a recommendation." Zapier and n8n do orchestration.

P

Parameter — A number inside an AI model that has been learned from training data. GPT-4 has trillions of parameters. More parameters usually means more capability, but also slower/more expensive to run.

Prompt — The instruction you give to an AI. "Summarize this email" or "Write a blog post about X." Good prompts are specific and show examples. Bad prompts are vague ("make it better").

Prompt Engineering — The skill of writing prompts that get better AI outputs. Includes techniques like giving examples (few-shot), step-by-step reasoning, or asking the AI to explain its thinking. Often more cost-effective than fine-tuning.

R

RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) — Showing an AI relevant documents, then asking it to answer based on those documents. "Here's our handbook, now answer this employee question." Reduces hallucinations and keeps AI knowledge current.

Reinforcement Learning — Training AI by rewarding good outputs and punishing bad ones. Used to fine-tune models after initial training. Expensive but makes AI more aligned with what you want.

S

Semantic Search — Finding relevant content by meaning, not keywords. "Show me articles about remote work policies" finds relevant results even if they don't use the phrase "remote work." Uses embeddings.

Sentiment Analysis — Determining if text is positive, negative, or neutral. "This product is amazing" = positive. Used for analyzing customer feedback, social media, reviews. Often good enough with modern LLMs.

System Prompt — Instructions you give to an AI about how to behave overall, separate from the specific task. Like "You are a CEO," then asking it to write an email. The system prompt sets tone and guardrails.

T

Temperature — A setting that controls how "creative" or "random" an AI's outputs are. Low temperature (0.3) = consistent, logical. High temperature (1.0) = creative, varied. Set lower for business use cases.

Token — A chunk of text (roughly 4 characters) that AI processes. "Hello world" is about 2 tokens. When APIs charge per token, longer documents cost more. Context windows are measured in tokens.

Tool Use / Function Calling — Ability for an AI to call external functions or tools. "Here's a function that updates our database. Use it when the customer asks to change their settings." Makes AI more than just language generation.

Training Data — The information used to teach an AI model. GPT was trained on internet text up to 2021. Claude was trained on internet text, books, and code up to early 2024. Training data shapes what the AI knows and how it behaves.

Transformer — A type of neural network architecture that revolutionized AI. All modern LLMs use Transformers. You don't need to know how they work—just that they're powerful, require massive computing power, and scale well.

V

Vector Database — A database optimized for storing and searching embeddings. Instead of "find rows where name = 'Alice,'" you ask "find documents most similar to this concept." Useful for semantic search and RAG applications.

Vibe Coding — Building things without perfectly specifying every requirement upfront. You describe what you want, the AI builds it, you refine based on feel. Fast and creative but requires iteration. Good for prototypes, risky for precision work.

Vision Model — An AI that can understand images. GPT-4 with vision, Claude, Gemini can all read images. Useful for analyzing screenshots, documents, charts, or photographs. Text-only models can't see images.

W

Workflow Automation — Connecting multiple tools so that actions in one tool trigger actions in another. "When a new lead comes in, create a row in the database, send an email, and create a calendar reminder." Zapier and n8n excel at this.

Z

Zero-Shot Learning — Asking an AI to do something with no examples. "Categorize this email as urgent/important/info without any examples." Less reliable than few-shot but faster when you don't have examples.
